The Man who Ignited my Code

A tribute to my mentor who transformed my curiosity into structured learning. His early encouragement shaped the developer I’m becoming.


I was just a curious learner fumbling through HTML tags and CSS rules, endless youtube videos, not entirely sure where I was headed. That’s when I met the man who would reshape my learning journey — Chukwuebuka Daniel Edeh.


He wasn’t just any mentor; he was the kind that calls you out when you’re not being consistent, the kind that gives you a real-world project even when you don’t feel ready. One project I’ll never forget — was a simple broker website. To me, it felt like a mountain.
I struggled to understand how to structure it, how to connect styles, how to think like a developer. But instead of handing me all the answers, he asked the right questions and guided me just enough to think independently.


Even when I disappointed myself by missing deadlines, he didn’t discard me. He told me, “You are more than this, and you know it.” That phrase stuck with me and shaped the way I now approach learning — not as a race, but a journey of continuous improvement.


Today, every project I touch, every bug I fix, and every skill I build echoes back to that one man who chose to mentor me with both patience and fire. He ignited my code — and I’m forever grateful.

From Curiosity to Code

How I started from zero, pushed through countless bugs, and finally launched my first fully functional app.


Before I became someone who could build functional web apps and write JavaScript logic with confidence, I was just a curious guy who asked, “How do websites actually work?”


That curiosity led me to learn HTML. I remember being amazed at how a few lines of code could turn into a visible webpage or the first time I made a css styling apply to a pragraph text . Then came CSS — colors, layouts, positioning — and the real headache: responsiveness. But I stuck with it. I watched tutorials, followed walkthroughs, and Googled a lot.


Then I met JavaScript. That’s when everything started making sense and getting chaotic at the same time. I remember working on loops, functions, and DOM manipulation. It felt like learning a new language — not just technically, but mentally. Still, I kept pushing.


One of my biggest wins was building an Expense Tracker from scratch. It started as just another project. But for the first time, I combined everything I had learned: HTML, CSS, JavaScript — even localStorage. I was writing logic I could explain, code I could debug, and features I could proudly show off.


That moment I saw the tracker update in real time — incomes and expenses neatly summarized, data stored locally — I knew this wasn’t just a hobby anymore. I was becoming a developer.

Beginner Developer Tip: What I Wish I Knew When I Started

A list of practical tips for beginners — including mistakes I made, tools I wish I learned earlier, and how I overcame discouragement;


As I continued my journey, I faced Hard truths, received honest advice, and learnt lessons early enough in becoming a developer


Now, if I could go back and have a coffee chat with my beginner self, here’s what I’d say:


1. Projects matter more than tutorials: Tutorials give you theory, but projects give you problems to solve. My biggest growth happened when I built things like the Expense Tracker. That’s where real understanding came from — not from watching, but from doing.


2. Your code doesn’t have to be perfect — just functional: I used to waste hours trying to make something look or feel “right” instead of getting it working. Now I follow the MVP mindset — launch something basic, improve it later.


3. Don’t be afraid of bugs — be excited by them: Bugs used to scare me. Now I see them as puzzles. The satisfaction of fixing a bug you’ve been stuck on all day is unmatched. It’s part of the thrill of coding.


4. Know your tools: Learning how to use DevTools, localStorage, and console logs changed the way I troubleshoot. These tools are your allies — master them early.


5. Community matters: Having a mentor, asking questions, getting feedback — these are the things that kept me going. You’re not alone, and you’ll go faster when you’re not coding in a vacuum. If you're just starting out, know this: every expert you admire was once confused too. Keep showing up. Keep building.


Inside My Expense Tracker

A breakdown of my first real-world web app. See how I built it with JavaScript, structured my logic, and added local storage support


The Expense Tracker was more than just a school assignment or a coding drill — it was the first time I turned an idea into something fully functional.


I started by designing the UI wireframe with sections for income, expenses, and a summary display. Then I moved to HTML and CSS to create the structure and layout. The real magic, however, was in the JavaScript.


I wrote functions that took in user input, checked if the category was income or expense, calculated totals, and dynamically updated the DOM. It felt empowering to see the numbers update instantly as I typed and submitted.


But I didn’t stop there. I wanted persistence — so I added localStorage. That’s when I really understood how data can live in the browser.
I learned how to: Save each transaction using JSON.stringify() Retrieve them using JSON.parse() Dynamically display them on page load Update localStorage when a transaction is deleted


The first time I refreshed the page and my data stayed — that was a breakthrough moment. It may not have had a backend, but it had logic, interaction, and persistence. That project made me feel like a real developer. And the best part? I could explain every single line of code I wrote. Now, I’m applying the same approach to new projects — like this blog — and the future feels wide open.
